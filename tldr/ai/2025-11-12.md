[### Why Sudoku Variants Remain a Grand Challenge in AI Reasoning (15 minute read)](https://pub.sakana.ai/sudoku-gpt5/?utm_source=tldrai)

Modified versions of Sudoku test an AI's creativity and ability to strategize around rulesets that don't appear in its training data, unlike fixed-rule games like Chess or Go. GPT-5 is the first LLM to solve a 9x9 variant by simultaneously coordinating four constraint types (standard rules, region sum lines, XV pairs, and Roman numeral cages), but it still only succeeded 20% of the time.

[### From Words to Worlds: Spatial Intelligence is AI's Next Frontier (18 minute read)](https://drfeifei.substack.com/p/from-words-to-worlds-spatial-intelligence?utm_source=tldrai)

Current LLMs are wordsmiths in the dark‚Äîeloquent but inexperienced. They lack a core cognitive function of humans: spatial intelligence. "World models" solve this by generating geometrically and physically consistent simulated worlds, processing multimodal inputs like images and gestures, and predicting next states based on actions. If successful, the technology would be a step change for robotics, video models, and drug discovery.

[### RL Environments and the Hierarchy of Agentic Capabilities (23 minute read)](https://surgehq.ai/blog/rl-envs-real-world?utm_source=tldrai)

Researchers dropped nine frontier AI models into a simulated workplace environment and gave them 150 jobs to do. Most of the AIs were barely coherent, and even the best lacked common sense. The experiment showed how models must acquire more fundamental capabilities before we can even begin to discuss how well they perform common-sense reasoning in real environments. Finding out whether common sense reasoning is a set of identifiable, trainable sub-skills or an emergent property of large-scale real-world training will help shape the next stage of AI development.
