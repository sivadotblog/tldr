Daniel Litt argues that current limitations are real but likely temporary, not fundamental blockers. Litt now expects AI to autonomously resolve mildly interesting open conjectures as early as late 2026 and suggests AI's long-term impact on mathematics may exceed that of the computer. Even in a future where machines can generate vast bodies of correct math, he argues human mathematicians will still matter because the core motivation remains understanding, not just producing results.

[### The First Fully General Computer Action Model (14 minute read)](https://si.inc/posts/fdm1/?utm_source=tldrai)

FDM-1, a foundation model for computer use, efficiently trains on 11 million hours of video to autonomously handle tasks like CAD, driving, and fuzzing. Unlike traditional models that rely on expensive, limited annotation, FDM-1 uses a video encoder that compresses 2 hours of video into 1M tokens and predicts actions with inverse dynamics for scalable training. With enhanced context processing, FDM-1 showcases significant advancements in executing long-horizon tasks and exploring complex state trees in GUI environments.

[### Self-Aware Reasoning Efficiency (9 minute read)](https://hzx122.github.io/sage-rl/?utm_source=tldrai)

SAGE introduces a self-aware sampling approach that enables large reasoning models to decide when to stop thinking, reducing unnecessary computation. Its companion method, SAGE-RL, distills these efficient reasoning patterns into standard pass@1 inference, reporting accuracy improvement with fewer tokens across six challenging math benchmarks.

[### Taalas HC1: Absurdly Fast, Per-User Inference at 17,000 tokens/second (5 minute read)](https://kaitchup.substack.com/p/taalas-hc1-absurdly-fast-per-user?utm_source=tldrai)
